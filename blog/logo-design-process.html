My Logo Design Process — From Brief to Final Files

By Usman G Custom Logo Designer 5 min read

Clients often ask what actually happens between sending me a brief and receiving their finished logo. So instead of just describing it, here's the real five-stage process I follow on every custom logo design project — the same process behind every piece in my portfolio, from streetwear marks to gothic monograms.

Watch the stages cycle below, then read the breakdown of each one underneath.

A visual walkthrough of the 5-stage process — loops automatically

01 Discovery & Brief

Every project starts with understanding the business, not opening design software. I ask about the target audience, competitors, the feeling the brand needs to communicate, and where the logo will actually be used — merchandise, app icon, storefront, packaging. Skipping this step is how designers end up producing something technically nice but strategically wrong.

  • What does the brand sell, and to who specifically?
  • What competitors already exist in that space, and what do their logos look like?
  • Where will the logo live — apparel tags, social avatars, packaging, signage?

02 Sketch Concepts

Before touching a vector tool, I sketch multiple rough directions by hand or in a simple digital sketchpad. This stage is intentionally messy — the goal is exploring shape and concept fast, not polish. Most of the ideas that end up in a final logo were never the first sketch; they came from the third or fourth attempt.

03 Digital Refinement

The strongest sketch direction gets rebuilt properly in vector software, where shapes get mathematically precise — clean curves, consistent stroke weights, and correct proportions replace the rough hand-drawn lines. This is where a concept starts looking like a professional logo design instead of a doodle.

04 Color & Typography

Color and type get selected deliberately, not by preference. Dark, saturated tones read as authority; bright, saturated colors read as energy — which is why streetwear brands and law firms almost never share a palette. Typography follows the same logic: a gothic blackletter reads completely differently than a clean modern sans-serif, even paired with an identical icon.

05 Final Vector Delivery

Once approved, the final logo gets exported into every format a real business actually needs — not just one flat image. That means vector AI/SVG/EPS files for scaling and printing, transparent PNGs for digital use, and print-ready PDFs, plus single-color versions for embroidery and low-cost merch printing.

A logo isn't finished when it looks good — it's finished when it works everywhere your brand needs it to.

That's the real process behind every piece in my portfolio — no shortcuts, no drag-and-drop templates, and no logo leaves my hands until it passes the same five stages every single time.
